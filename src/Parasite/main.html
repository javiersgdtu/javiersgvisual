"A zeitgeist-defining sensation". That's how the Criterion collection speaks about 2019 Academy award winner film Parasite. The film not only achieved recognition across world critique but its thrilling suspense plot captivated mainstream audience.

In PARASITE we are introduced to a low-class family, the Kims, that slyly infiltrates in the lives of the wealthy Parks. One of the most impressive aspects of this class inequality tale is its use of space.

66% of the film takes place in the Parks’ house. The impressive household was built for the occasion in a one piece set by Production designer Lee Ha Jun. Wide rooms, endless stairs and secret passages: a maze designed for the characters to traverse.

This house plays a key defining role and the way that the protagonists inhabit it tells a lot about them. In this visualization, we try to understand the importance of architecture in the film and how it embodies characters' class and intention.

How does this building shapes the characters? What does the way they move and the rooms they stay reveals?

" The vertical structure of the house reflects the relations between the three families, with Geunse at the bottom. Whilst Park 's family mostly only go up the stairs, Kim and Geunse' s families move up and down repeatedly. That 's the essence of this film." . Lee Ha Jun - Interview with dezeen.


The character that introduces us to the house is Ki Woo, likewise he is the character with more screen time. If we counted the time that we see all the characters in the house, he accounts for a 15% of the total.
During scene 5, the Kims fantasize with the idea of being the owners of the house. Paradoxically, on a family level, they are the ones driving more screen time (47%) at the alien Parks’ house.
What it is more interesting is to understand what areas of the house the characters occupy.

Occupation

As we descend in the house levels, Parks occupation decreases. They rarely descend to the lower levels of the House and the Street, leaving that for the lower social classes.

On the contrary, Geun Se and Moon Gwang gain weight there. They barely reach to the second level of the house.

Occupation

The Kims have an even distribution among the house levels (~50%). This portrays how well they have played their cards in this hoax. Coming from a poor background they reach the higher levels of the house without much complications.

Though as the film goes by, they tend to lead to the lower levels of the house (50% in the 6th moment, 42% in the 7th & 100% on the last moment). This shows the instability of their game.

Movement

Previously we saw the different classifications made depending on Characters actions: Up, Down, Move, Static & Hiding. Here, we summarize the character traits depending on how they move.

Movement

Up/Down

The characters that go up/down more often are Geun Se and Moon Gwang. At the beggining because they work for the Parks and later trying to escape from the Bunker.

In the underground, The low-class families (Kims and Geun Ses) move up and down the most trying to escalate as much as possible from inferior levels.

Movement

Static

Across the film, Parks and Geun Se’s families tend to be the more Static. Though a closer look on their occupation reveals a different reality.

Parks tend to be static in the commodity of the Second floor. Whereas Geun Se and Moon Gwang stay static the longest on the lower levels of the house, specially on the underground bunker where they are locked later in the film.

The first floor works as a melting point of static characters.

Movement

Move

In he second floor, the Kim family moves quite a lot, whereas the Parks tend to have a lower moving rate and high static rate. This floor, the uppest one, is as a symbol of social recognision, the Kims live in a mirage in the house.

On the contrary in the street and underground Kims and Geun Se' s are the most dinamyc.

Methodology

The first Visual is inspired in the Parasite's Criterion Collection DVD/Bluray edition. The extracts of interviews were obtained from deezen and Indiwire interviews with Parasite's production designer Lee Ha Jun. The inspiration of the whole scrollytelling article is The Pudding visual essays.
The data visualized was obtained from a Database that I created addressing moments in the film. Please, if you use it for your projects quote me. I am looking forward to see the possibilities that it offers!
The film takes place in different locations: Parks' house (66%), Kims' house (14%)... (you can see them all in the dataset). Here we only focus in the Park's house. In the Locations mode we brush in a 0 to 100 timeline. This range represents the total time that the house is in screen (rather than the duration of the film).
There were some locations in the house (the toilet & sauna room) that were not included in the house visualization, as it was not clear where they are located and represented a minor time screen (less than 1%).
